# Evidence — the epistemic contract, tiers, sources, and standing caveats

This is the discipline that makes the method defensible rather than merely plausible. Every load-bearing claim in the skill and in the campaign document carries a class. Nothing gets dressed as research that isn't. Consult this file when a claim needs its receipt, when the campaign document will face a board, or when the user challenges grounding.

## The claim classes

| Tag | Class | Meaning | How to treat it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **K** | Known | Supported by replicated, sourced empirical research | Rely on it — but still context-check against this case |
| **A** | Assumed | Established practitioner convention; influential, not experimentally proven | Use as the default; drop it if the case argues otherwise |
| **I** | Inferred | Follows logically from a K/A claim or accepted theory | Reason from it; watch the size of the leap |
| **S** | Speculative | Synthesis or judgment | A hypothesis to test, never a finding |

## Evidence tiers

**Tier 1 — genuinely empirical.** Large datasets, replicated. Primarily the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute (Univ. of South Australia) and the IPA (UK). The closest marketing gets to hard evidence — and it is *observational*, not experimental.

**Tier 2 — respected but not peer-reviewed, or academic-adjacent.** Influential theory and craft canon (Christensen/Harvard; Heath/Stanford; Ogilvy; account planning; Porter/Harvard; behavioural economics).

**Tier 3 — synthesis.** Logical, defensible, unstudied. Tagged [S] wherever it appears; includes the six-step sequence and ordering, franchise-vs-firework framing, the opposite test, the Register test and doctrine, the first domino, and the tease→afterglow arc labels.

## Tier 1 sources

- **Binet, L. & Field, P. (2013). *The Long and the Short of It.* IPA.** ~1,000 IPA Databank cases (1980–2010). Used for: emotional brand-building outperforms rational work long-term; ~60:40 brand/activation as profit-optimal split; creative fame pays only when correctly branded.
- **Danenberg, N., Kennedy, R., Beal, V. & Sharp, B. (2016). *Journal of Advertising.*** ESOV growth relationship (~+0.5–0.7% market share/year per 10 points of excess share of voice). The concept traces to John Philip Jones's share-of-voice work.
- **Sharp, B. (2010). *How Brands Grow.* OUP.** Mental and physical availability as growth drivers; growth through penetration.
- **Sharp, B. & Romaniuk, J. (2015). *How Brands Grow: Part 2.* OUP.**
- **Romaniuk, J. (2018). *Building Distinctive Brand Assets.* OUP.** Assets scored on fame × uniqueness; consistency compounds recognition.

## Tier 2 sources

- **Heath, C. & Heath, D. (2007). *Made to Stick.*** SUCCESs checklist; "find the core." A synthesis of existing psychology, not an experiment of its own — the "Stanford" anchor, honestly qualified.
- **Christensen, C. M. — Jobs-to-Be-Done** (e.g., *Competing Against Luck*, 2016). Insight as the job a customer hires a product to do. Influential theory, not a controlled finding — the "Harvard" anchor, honestly qualified.
- **Porter, M. E. (1996). "What Is Strategy?" *HBR.*** Strategy as the discipline of sacrifice.
- **Ogilvy, D. (1983). *Ogilvy on Advertising*; Young, J. W. (1965). *A Technique for Producing Ideas.*** Idea before tactics; the big idea. Canon, not tested.
- **Account-planning tradition** (Stephen King, JWT; Stanley Pollitt, BMP). Insight as tension. Craft discipline, widely held, not experimentally validated.
- **Google/YouTube (2014).** Hero-Hub-Hygiene. Platform/practitioner model.
- **Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*; Thaler, R.** Behavioural levers beneath persuasion.

## The three standing caveats

These apply to everything the skill produces and are the mark of real rigour. Carry them into any document that will face scrutiny.

**1 · Observational, not experimental.** The Tier-1 evidence comes from case-study and award databanks — and the IPA data is drawn from *award-winning* campaigns, which biases the sample upward. Strong, repeatedly observed patterns, not physical laws. Use them to shift the odds, not to guarantee outcomes.

**2 · FMCG-derived evidence vs. premium niches.** Sharp's work — the backbone of the distinctiveness and franchise logic — was built largely on mass-market, repeat-purchase FMCG. Its core claims (grow by penetration, distinctiveness over differentiation) sit in genuine tension with premium, niche, considered products whose thesis is differentiation and a devoted base. Apply Ehrenberg-Bass selectively: keep the discipline of ownable, consistent distinctive assets (which serves such brands), hold lightly the penetration-over-everything doctrine (which may not). Name the tension rather than quoting Sharp at a brand he never studied.

**3 · No formula for a great idea.** Every Tier-1 finding describes the *conditions* under which campaigns pay off — emotion, consistency, correct branding, disproportionate presence. None manufactures the creative leap. Anything promising a validated recipe for "astonishing" is selling folklore in a lab coat. The method stacks the conditions and disciplines the judgment; it does not replace the judgment.

## Using the contract in campaign documents

- Tag load-bearing claims in the ledger section; leave routine craft untagged rather than tag-spamming the document.
- No tag upgrades without a reason recorded in the ledger (e.g., an [S] assumption validated by a test becomes [K-local] — known for this brand, from its own data, which is the strongest evidence a small brand ever gets).
- No [S] claim may carry the campaign's central argument to a board; if one does, say so and present it as the hypothesis being bought.
