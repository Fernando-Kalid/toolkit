# The Discriminating Tests — the quality gate

Run every candidate idea through all seven, in writing, before it reaches the user. The gate exists because attractive ideas fail quietly: they feel right in the room and dissolve on contact with the market. An idea that fails one test is not "close" — it is a different, weaker idea wearing your idea's clothes. The honest verdicts here are what make Gate B a real decision instead of theater.

## Scoring protocol

For each test write **PASS** or **FAIL** plus one line of evidence — the specific reason, not a restatement of the question. No "partial." If you're tempted to write "borderline," that's a FAIL with an explanation. Any FAIL sends the candidate back to Step 3 for repair or replacement; record repaired candidates as such so the user sees the idea's history. The completed scorecard ships inside the campaign document (template C2).

## The seven tests

### 1 · Ownership — does it die when the logo swaps?
Swap in the nearest competitor's logo. If the idea still works, it's a category ad — generic goodwill anyone can harvest. A real campaign idea *dies* when you change the brand, because it's built from this brand's specific position, codes, and refusals.
**How to judge:** actually perform the swap in one sentence ("[Competitor] presents: [platform]") and read it aloud. If nothing breaks, FAIL.
**Grounding:** [K — distinctiveness: Sharp 2010; Romaniuk 2018]

### 2 · Sacrifice — what does it refuse?
Name what this campaign refuses to say, show, or chase. No refusal, no shape — and for a restraint brand, the refusal is often the source of the power.
**How to judge:** the refusal must be something a competent competitor *would plausibly do* — refusing something nobody would do anyway is not sacrifice.
**Grounding:** [K — Porter 1996]

### 3 · Branding — does the fame attach?
After exposure, do people remember the *brand and its role* — or just the spectacle? Fame that doesn't attach to the brand is expensive entertainment. Strip the logo: is it still obviously this brand, because it's built on the brand's codes?
**How to judge:** describe the hero execution to an imagined stranger, logo removed. If they could plausibly attribute it to three brands, FAIL.
**Grounding:** [K — Binet & Field 2013: creative fame pays only when correctly branded]

### 4 · Insight/Tension — does it resolve something real?
Does the idea resolve a genuine tension in the audience's life (the job they'd hire the product for)? Apply the opposite test from Step 2: state the insight, state its opposite. Opposite obviously stupid → platitude → FAIL. Opposite also arguably true → real insight.
**Grounding:** [S] formulation over [I — Christensen, Jobs-to-Be-Done]

### 5 · Fertility — firework or flywheel?
Can the idea run three times and get *richer*, or does it exhaust on first contact? Franchises compound mental availability; fireworks win a week.
**How to judge:** demand the third execution, not the first. If executions two and three are strictly weaker echoes of execution one, FAIL.
**Grounding:** [K — Sharp/Romaniuk: consistency compounds]

### 6 · The Tuesday Test — can you build it?
Does the idea instantly generate concrete executions — can you picture a specific post on a specific Tuesday? Or is it a beautiful slogan you can't build?
**How to judge:** write one execution with a date, a channel, and a format in a single sentence. If that sentence is hard to write, FAIL.
**Grounding:** [A/I — Heath: concreteness]

### 7 · Register — does the mode of astonishment match the brand?
See the doctrine below. A technically excellent idea in the wrong register fails this test outright.
**Grounding:** [S — judgment, and the most protective test for premium/restraint brands]

## The Register doctrine

*Tagged [S] throughout — the most useful part of the gate and the least "proven." Treat it as a strong hypothesis, not a finding.*

The trap in the word *astonishing* is that it defaults to *loud*. For a loud DTC challenger, astonishing may genuinely mean a stunt. For a quiet-luxury, analog, anti-fragmentation object, astonishing means depth, precision, intimacy, patience, and the confidence to under-say. The boldest move available to a restraint brand is restraint executed at a level no one else will bother with: a single, near-perfect object, shot with obsessive care, saying almost nothing.

To apply it: first name the brand's register in one line, derived from the strategy's ontology and semiotic system (loud/challenger · warm/communal · precise/expert · quiet/luxury · playful/irreverent — or whatever the strategy itself implies). Then ask whether the *mechanism of surprise* in the idea belongs to that register. A loud execution under an anti-noise thesis actively contradicts the product; a whisper from a challenger brand reads as weakness, not confidence. The effect to aim for, for a restraint brand: the astonishment of walking into a nearly empty room and realising every remaining thing is exactly right.

## Using this file in Idea-audit mode

When the user brings an existing idea: reconstruct the implied brief first (objective, segment, belief-move as best you can infer — tag inferences [I], gaps [S]), because tests 1, 3, and 7 are unjudgeable without knowing the brand's codes and register. Then run all seven, return the scorecard, name the *diagnosis* (which failure is load-bearing) and the single highest-leverage repair. Resist rewriting the whole idea unless asked.
