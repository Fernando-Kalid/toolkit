# The Six Steps — expanded construction guidance

This file carries the depth behind SKILL.md's step summaries: how to construct each output, the evidence-anchored numbers with their honest caveats, and the failure modes to avoid. Every load-bearing claim keeps its evidence tag — [K] known/replicated, [A] practitioner convention, [I] inferred, [S] speculative synthesis. Full tiers, sources, and standing caveats in `evidence.md`.

## Step 1 — Compile the brief onto one card

This is not the strategy in miniature; it is a **decision about where to concentrate fire**. The card answers exactly:

- **Objective** — the single job of *this* campaign (awareness / first sales / list-building / shifting one specific belief). One, not a menu.
- **Segment** — one audience, drawn from the strategy and *narrowed further*. "Our audience" is not a campaign segment.
- **Territory** — which one of the strategy's territories is this campaign's home. A secondary territory may support; it may not co-star.
- **Arc** — which strategic arc this campaign advances. A campaign that advances no arc is an orphan; it may still sell, but it doesn't compound.
- **Belief-move** — one pair, written as a falsifiable sentence: *"from [current belief] to [target belief], for [this person]."* This sentence **is** the campaign's causal thesis. If it can't be false, it can't be a thesis.
- **The number** — the one metric that would prove the belief moved. Pick it now; Step 6 will interrogate it.

**Heuristic: one campaign, one job, one number.** [A — practitioner discipline, reinforced by the evidence that simplicity and relentless prioritisation are what make a message land: I — Heath & Heath 2007, "find the core"]

**How to choose when the strategy offers riches:** pick the objective the *business* needs most in this window (the strategy's commercial analysis names it), then the belief-move that most directly serves that objective, then the territory where that belief-move has the most cultural energy right now. Objective → belief → territory, in that order — choosing a favorite territory first and retrofitting an objective is decoration, not concentration.

**Failure modes:** three objectives in a trench coat; a "belief-move" that is actually a slogan; a number the team cannot measure; a segment so broad the insight in Step 2 has no one specific to be true of.

## Step 2 — Mine the insight before inventing anything

An insight is not a feature or a fact. It is a **tension in the audience's life that the brand can resolve** — equivalently, the job they are hiring the product to do. [I — Christensen, Jobs-to-Be-Done] The idea built in Step 3 is the *resolution* of that tension in a form only this brand could stage.

**Where to mine:** the strategy already did the excavation — its cultural tensions and belief field, its behavior model (frictions, current practice in the audience's own logic), its commercial analysis (why people actually pay). Pull the tension that sits at the intersection of the chosen segment and the chosen belief-move. If the user has verbatims — reviews, support tickets, DMs, sales-call notes — mine those for the audience's *own words*; the insight lands harder phrased in their language than in strategy language.

**The opposite test.** State the insight, then state its opposite. If the opposite is obviously stupid ("people want to be disorganised"), you have a platitude. If the opposite is *also arguably true*, you have a real insight. [S — formulation; echoes the falsifiability principle of the strategy system's ledger]

**Output:** one insight sentence you would build a year of work on — not a paragraph, not three candidates. The narrowing is the work.

## Step 3 — Crack the idea as a franchise, not a firework

The highest-leverage move in the method. Do not invent a clever one-off execution. Invent a **repeatable, ownable device** — a phrase, a ritual, a format, a visual code — that can run three times and get *richer* each time.

The reason is empirical, not aesthetic: brands grow by building mental availability, and **distinctive brand assets** (scored on *fame × uniqueness*) are the memory cues that build it. Consistency compounds; novelty every cycle throws that compounding away. [K — Sharp 2010; Romaniuk 2018] Fireworks win a week; franchises build a brand.

**Construction rules:**

- **Extend existing distinctive assets, don't replace them.** If the strategy names a tagline, ritual, or visual code as an asset, the campaign platform should be built *on* it — the campaign then deposits equity into an account that already exists. [I — from the K claim above]
- **Compress to one sentence.** If the platform needs a paragraph, it isn't landed yet. [I — Heath & Heath 2007: find the core, make it compact]
- **Prove fertility with three concrete executions.** Not variations of one execution — three different moments the device generates. This is the difference between a platform and a slogan.
- **Make the candidates genuinely different.** 2–3 candidates should be different *resolutions of the tension* (e.g., one ritual-based, one format-based, one artifact-based), not three phrasings of one idea. Genuine alternatives make Gate B a real choice.

**Franchise starting shapes** (prompts, not a menu to pick from blindly): a named recurring ritual (a day, an hour, a practice the brand owns); a countable series (No. 001, 002…); a repeatable format (same structure, new subject each time); a physical or digital artifact that accumulates; a phrase that completes differently each time. The best device usually *stages the insight* — it makes the audience perform the resolution, not just watch it.

**No formula caveat:** everything above stacks the conditions for a great idea; none of it manufactures the leap. If every candidate feels dead, the problem is usually upstream — reopen Step 2, not the thesaurus. [See evidence.md, caveat 3]

## Step 5 — Architect the ecosystem and the arc

Once the idea is locked, build the system around it — and break the instinct to spread evenly.

- **Assign each channel one job.** Not "be everywhere" — this one builds the belief, this one converts, this one answers search. A channel with two jobs does neither. [A — media-planning convention]
- **Split brand-building vs. activation.** Reference point: across ~1,000 IPA cases the profit-optimal split was roughly **60% brand-building / 40% activation**, with emotional brand work outperforming rational work long-term. [K — Binet & Field 2013] *Caveat:* FMCG/B2C-derived; the same authors' B2B work found ~46:54. For a premium, considered, founder-led product, treat 60:40 as a starting anchor that likely tilts *further* toward brand, and validate against results. [I + caveat] State the chosen split and its reasoning in the document.
- **Concentrate, don't dilute.** Pour disproportionate resource behind one hero moment; let everything else orbit it. The underlying principle — being disproportionately present relative to your size drives growth — is quantified in paid media as roughly +0.5–0.7% market share per year per 10 points of excess share of voice. [K — Danenberg, Kennedy, Beal & Sharp, *Journal of Advertising*, 2016] *Caveat:* ESOV is a paid-media concept; with a near-zero media budget the literal maths doesn't apply, but the direction does — concentration of attention and consistency of presence substitute for ad dollars. [I + caveat]
- **Structure the content ecosystem** as a few **hero** tentpole moments, regular **hub** content developing the idea, and always-on **hygiene**/utility content. [A — Google/YouTube "Hero-Hub-Hygiene," 2014]
- **Sequence the arc in time:** tease → reveal → sustain → convert → afterglow, with dates. [A/S — a sequencing heuristic descended from AIDA; no strong empirical pedigree, hold it loosely — collapse or reorder phases when the case argues for it]
- **Name the first domino.** The single launch act that, if it lands, pulls the rest into motion. Naming it forces the team to know what actually starts the campaign — especially load-bearing for tiny teams. [S]

**Failure modes:** even spreading (the ecosystem as a checklist of channels); a "hero moment" that is just the biggest item on a list rather than the thing everything else orbits; an arc with phases but no dates; channel jobs that all say "awareness."

## Step 6 — Instrument it, then protect scope

- **Pre-register kill and amplify criteria before launch:** what week-one (or first-window) signals say *stop* versus *pour fuel*, with thresholds and dates. Falsification conditions written after the fact will be set so the campaign can't fail. [K — pre-registration as anti-bias methodology; native to the strategy system's proof module]
- **Separate leading from lagging.** The number from Step 1 is usually lagging (sales, sign-ups). Identify the early signals that *predict* it (saves, replies, qualified traffic, waitlist velocity) and wire the kill/amplify thresholds to those.
- **Protect scope brutally.** A small team cannot execute a sprawling campaign well. Over-invest in the hero moment; cut the rest without mercy. List what was cut — the cuts are evidence the concentration is real. [S/A — constraint made into a virtue]
- **Close the loop with the strategy.** Whatever the campaign learns about the belief-move is evidence for or against the strategy's causal thesis. Note in the document what result would warrant reopening which strategy module — the campaign is also an experiment the strategy runs on reality.
