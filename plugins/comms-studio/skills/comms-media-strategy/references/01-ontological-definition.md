# Module 1 — Ontological Definition

**Role in the system:** establishes what the entity *is*, before anyone argues about what it should say.

## Purpose

Define the entity's nature with enough precision that every downstream module works on the same object: its kind, its category logic, its role in people's lives, its claim to existence, and the distinction between what it sells and what it socially represents. Most bad strategies are bad here first — they strategize for a fantasy entity, or for the wrong layer of a real one.

## Invoke when

Starting any full build (co-iterating with Module 6); when a user asks "what actually is this brand?"; when a strategy keeps drifting because stakeholders hold different unspoken definitions of the entity; when evaluating a rebrand, extension, or category move.

## Required inputs

- The entity as the user describes it, plus its **layer** (campaign / product / product line / brand / business model / institution — an entity is usually a stack; name which layer this strategy serves).
- Whatever material reality exists: what is actually sold, to whom, at what price, through what channel.
- Draft exchange from Module 6 when available (ontology and economics co-determine each other).

## Expected outputs — the Ontology Brief

1. **Entity kind and layer.** What sort of thing this is, and which layer of the stack the strategy addresses.
2. **Category logic.** What mental shelf people currently place it on; whether the strategy should *accept* that category (win within its rules), *reframe* it (change the comparison set), or *create* one (pay category-education costs). Name the price of the chosen move.
3. **Role in life.** The functional job, the social role, and the identity resource it offers — kept distinct.
4. **Claim to existence.** Why the world should contain this entity. "To make money" is the owner's reason, not the world's. Test: *if it disappeared tomorrow, who would miss it, and what exactly would they miss?*
5. **Sells vs. represents.** The commercial object versus the social/cultural object (the SUV sells transport; it represents competent guardianship). Both named, neither collapsed into the other.
6. **Negative ontology.** What the entity is *not* — the two or three nearest confusable entities and the line that separates it from each.

## Quality bar

The **stranger test**: an intelligent stranger reading the Brief could say what this is, for whom, and why it exists — in one paragraph, without a single marketing word. Every claim about what the entity "represents" is tagged in the ledger (most start as A or I, not K).

## Definition of success

Downstream modules stop re-litigating identity. Module 4 knows which beliefs matter, Module 6 knows what the margin is rented from, Module 7 inherits a positioning candidate it can sharpen rather than invent.

## Failure modes

- **Category tautology.** "A great X for people who want great X" — restates the shelf without defining the object.
- **Aspirational inflation.** Declaring the beverage "a movement." Ontology describes what the entity *is and can currently sustain being*; aspiration belongs in Module 7 as a chosen bet, ledger-tagged.
- **Ontology by committee.** A composite of every stakeholder's fantasy — internally contradictory and therefore useless as a foundation.
- **Layer confusion.** Writing brand-level ontology for a campaign brief, or campaign-level ontology for a business model.

## Never do

- Never import positioning or persuasive language — this module describes; Module 7 persuades.
- Never assert cultural meanings as facts without handing them to Modules 2 and 4 for tagging and verification.
- Never resolve a real contradiction in the entity by wording around it. Contradictions are findings; report them.

## Handoff

Complete when the Brief passes the stranger test and its claims are ledger-tagged. Hands to: Module 6 (co-iteration partner), then Modules 4, 3, and 7. If Module 6's economics contradict the claimed ontology (the "craft" brand whose economics require industrial scale), the contradiction goes to the ledger as a strategic risk — it is Module 7's problem to resolve, honestly.

## Output shape

```
ONTOLOGY BRIEF — [entity], [layer]
Kind & layer:        …
Category logic:      current shelf → accept / reframe / create + cost of the move
Role in life:        functional … | social … | identity …
Claim to existence:  … (disappearance test result)
Sells:               …          Represents: …
Is not:              [near-entity 1] because … | [near-entity 2] because …
Ledger tags:         [claim → K/A/I/S]
Open contradictions: …
```
