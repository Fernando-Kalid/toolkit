# Module 5 — Anthropological & Behavioral Influence

**Role in the system:** converts cultural understanding and commercial need into a model of what people actually do, what must change, and which levers move it — legitimately.

## Purpose

Ground the strategy in observed practice rather than professed attitude, then engineer the change: name the target behavior precisely, inventory the frictions that block it, and design levers that work *with* the person's own logic. The module's ethic is structural, not cosmetic: influence that survives being explained to the person it is used on.

## Invoke when

After Module 4 (cultural warrant for levers) and Module 6's draft Leverage Map (economic value of behaviors) exist; when a user asks how to change usage, switching, adoption, adherence, or advocacy; when a strategy is long on messaging and short on any account of why anyone would act.

## Required inputs

- Cultural Field Map + Belief Delta (Module 4).
- Leverage Map draft (Module 6) — which behaviors are worth money.
- Behavioral evidence from intake: observed practice, funnel data, service anecdotes, ethnography if it exists. Where only attitude data exists, say so — the attitude–behavior gap is a standing finding, not a footnote.

## Expected outputs — the Behavior Model

1. **Current practice, thickly described.** What people actually do, in sequence, in context, *in their own logic* — the reasonable reasons behind "irrational" behavior. If the description would embarrass the people described, it is not yet understood.
2. **Target behavior specification.** Observable, specific, situated, dated. "Consideration" is not a behavior. "Bartender adds it to a named menu cocktail within 90 days of tasting" is.
3. **Friction inventory.** What blocks the target behavior, sorted honestly: *practical* (effort, cost, access, timing), *social* (what adopting signals to others; who is watching), *identity* (what adopting says about me to me). Identity frictions masquerade as practical objections; the model must call them by name.
4. **Lever design.** For each friction or dormant motivation, a lever — ordered by strength: context and default changes; incentives; ritual and habit anchors; identity and self-signaling; social proof; and messaging last. Messaging is the weakest lever and the most commonly overused; the model says plainly when the job belongs to product, price, or distribution instead (backflow to Module 6).
5. **Ethics screen, applied per lever.** Two tests, both mandatory: the **transparency test** — does the lever still work if the person understands exactly how it works? — and the **journalist test** — accurately described on a front page, is it a scandal? Levers failing either are removed, not disguised. No deception, no coercion, no dark patterns, no exploitation of vulnerability or manufactured anxiety.
6. **Warrants.** Each lever carries its cultural warrant (which field-map finding says this will resonate) and its economic value (which leverage-map line it moves).

## Quality bar

Target behaviors are countable by an observer. Every lever has both warrants attached and a passed ethics screen recorded. The friction inventory contains at least one finding the client will not enjoy hearing — a model with only convenient frictions hasn't looked.

## Definition of success

Module 7 can build objectives around behaviors that are worth money, culturally plausible, and honestly achievable. Module 9 inherits behavior metrics that measure change in the world rather than change in the dashboard.

## Failure modes

- **Attitude–behavior conflation.** Building on what people say rather than what they do.
- **Persuasion romanticism.** Assigning to messaging what context controls. Most "messaging problems" are distribution, default, or pricing problems wearing a costume.
- **Friction blindness.** Pouring motivation into a blocked pipe.
- **Segment fiction.** Personas invented for narrative convenience, unmoored from the value pools in Module 6.
- **Dark-pattern drift.** Levers that inch toward urgency theater, guilt mechanics, or confusion pricing. The ethics screen exists because drift is gradual.

## Never do

- Never recommend a mechanic that depends on the audience not understanding it.
- Never target a behavior the Leverage Map cannot connect to value — activity is not outcome.
- Never treat vulnerable states (grief, addiction, financial distress, body anxiety) as activation opportunities.

## Handoff

Complete when current practice, target behaviors, frictions, and screened levers are documented with warrants and tags. Hands to Module 7 (objectives and operating model) and Module 9 (behavioral metrics and experiments). If no ethical, plausible lever reaches a target behavior, that behavior returns to Modules 6/7 for re-selection — the system changes the ask, not the ethics.

## Output shape

```

CURRENT PRACTICE: [sequence, context, their logic] → tag
TARGET BEHAVIOR:  [observable act] by [who] in [situation] by [date] → value link: L#
FRICTIONS:  practical … | social … | identity …
LEVERS:
  [lever] → type: context/incentive/ritual/identity/proof/message
          → friction addressed … → cultural warrant: F# → economic value: L#
          → transparency test: pass | journalist test: pass
ESCALATIONS: [job that belongs to product/price/distribution → backflow note]
```
