# Module 8 — Executive Strategy Presentation

**Role in the system:** converts the audited strategy into a decision instrument for leadership. Not a summary — an argument with an ask.

## Purpose

Executives do not buy strategies; they buy arguments about money and risk that happen to contain strategies. This module builds that argument: business relevance first, the bet stated as a bet, the ask made unmissable — including the least-requested and most decisive ask, *protection*. Strategies rarely die from disapproval; they die from unprotected sacrifice, when month two arrives and someone senior asks for the thing the strategy refused.

## Invoke when

Only after Module 2's audit gate passes. Also standalone: converting an existing (externally produced) strategy into executive material — in which case run a fast audit first, because this module inherits the liability for any claim it presents.

## Required inputs

- The audited Strategy Document (Module 7) and Proof System (Module 9).
- The ledger — tags travel with claims into the room.
- Intake's decision context: who decides, what they care about, what they have been burned by before.

## Expected outputs

**The executive argument**, in whatever container the audience needs (memo, deck content, board paper — visual rendering hands off to `pptx` / `ophira-deck` / `success-planner-deck` / `docx` skills):

1. **Executive summary.** The situation, the bet, the money, the ask — one page, no throat-clearing.
2. **The argument arc.** Situation (material + cultural ground) → insight (what we see that others don't, with tags shown) → the bet (thesis in plain commercial language) → the plan (operating model, only at decision altitude) → the proof (how we'll know, when, and what kills it) → the ask.
3. **Decision logic.** Exactly what leadership must **approve** (the strategy and its refusals), **fund** (investment with rationale), and **protect** (the sacrifices under future pressure — named scenarios: "when sales asks for X in Q2, the answer this strategy requires is no").
4. **Strategic choices and rejected alternatives.** The two or three roads not taken and why. Executives trust strategies that show their work; a strategy with no visible alternatives reads as either naïve or predetermined.
5. **Investment rationale.** Cost of the strategy against the cost of the status quo — the do-nothing number made explicit, because "no decision" always masquerades as free.
6. **Risks and mitigations.** The real ones, from the fragility map — not risk theater. Include the lag structure: when effects should appear, what the dashboard will look like while the strategy is working but not yet visible, and the pre-agreed reading of month three. Unset expectations about lag are how good strategies get killed early.
7. **The 90-day proof plan.** From Module 9: the earliest honest evidence, and the experiments already funded to convert A-tags to K.

## Quality bar

The ask is specific enough to be minuted. Money appears before meaning. Every number in the room carries its tag — an executive who asks "how do we know that?" gets the ledger answer, not improvisation. The document survives the **CFO test**: the toughest financial reader finds the causal chain from spend to value without a leap of faith.

## Definition of success

Leadership can say yes to a specific thing, no to specific alternatives, and knows what it has promised to defend. Approval, funding, and protection are each explicitly obtained — or explicitly declined, which is also success: a clean no now beats a slow death at month four.

## Failure modes

- **Deck poetry.** Atmosphere replacing argument; the strategy hiding behind its own aesthetics.
- **The buried ask.** Forty minutes of context, ninety seconds of decision.
- **Podium invention.** New claims, promises, or numbers appearing at presentation that exist nowhere in the ledger. This module renders; it does not author.
- **Overpromised timelines.** Brand effects sold on performance-marketing schedules — buying approval now at the price of credibility later.
- **Risk theater.** A risks slide engineered to reassure, defeating its function and the audit behind it.

## Never do

- Never present an S-tagged claim as fact; never hide a sacrifice to smooth the room.
- Never let rendering polish substitute for a failed audit — this module refuses unaudited input.
- Never make the recommendation without stating what would change it (the falsification conditions come along).

## Handoff

Terminal for the build, but hands the *protection contract* back to the operating model: the named sacrifices leadership agreed to defend, with scenarios. Visual production hands to the user's deck/document skills; content authority stays here.
