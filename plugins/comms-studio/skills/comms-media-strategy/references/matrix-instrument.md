# The Territories × Functions Matrix

The strategy's central planning surface when both forms are in play. Rows are territories (what we claim meaning in); columns are functions (what jobs communication performs); cells are where strategy becomes production. Its virtues are exactly the ones that earn the default grammar its status: everyone can read it, gaps and waste become visible at a glance, and every brief acquires an address.

## Deriving the axes

**Rows — territories** come from Module 7's positioning built on Module 4's field map: the bounded meaning claims the strategy has chosen, each with its carving derivation (which intersection of proof, audience, and business model created it). If the production model is periodic, rows may be pillar-coupled; if not, they remain meaning territories governing whatever is made, at whatever cadence.

**Columns — functions** come from Module 6's leverage map plus the recorded theory commitment. Funnel-committed cases derive stage jobs (reach / usefulness / trust / conversion, retention where the model demands it). Memory-refresh-committed cases derive reach + distinctiveness. B2B committee cases may use stakeholder-jobs as columns (champion / economic buyer / blocker). The columns are always *jobs with economic warrants* — whatever job-theory the case earned.

Weight the axes before filling cells: each function carries the economic weight of its leverage-map rows; each territory carries its cultural heat and positioning priority. Cell importance is roughly the product, adjusted by fit (some territories simply cannot do some jobs credibly — mark those cells structurally empty rather than failed).

## The cell contract

An occupied cell specifies:

- **Format or franchise** doing the work (a franchise may occupy one cell, a row, or a column — see below).
- **Weight** — share of production capacity and budget, justified by the cell's importance.
- **Owner and cadence.**
- **Proof hook** — which Module 9 indicator this cell feeds; a cell no metric watches is unaccountable.
- **Brief address** — "T2×F3" becomes the shorthand that keeps every piece of work strategically legible.

## Reading the matrix

- **Gaps:** high-weight empty cells are strategy holes — either fill them or downgrade the claim that made them heavy.
- **Waste:** occupied low-weight cells are cut candidates; production gravitates toward what is fun to make.
- **Refusals:** deliberately empty cells are decisions and get a reason — the matrix is a map of sacrifices as much as activity.
- **Balance over time:** cell weights drift as results arrive (Module 9's cadence reviews reallocate); the matrix is a living allocation table, not a launch poster.

## The franchise layer

Franchises are the repeatable occupants of cells. Portfolio logic: cover the high-weight cells with the *minimum viable number of formats* — serialization economics reward few formats run long over many run thin. The count cap is derived, never inherited: hours-per-episode × cadence must fit inside the team's real hours, with slack for the non-serial work the strategy also requires. A franchise earns its slot by (a) sitting on heavy cells, (b) being repeatable without its inventor, and (c) building audience expectation — the container brief ("episode 12 of X") is the creativity technology that makes production scale.

## Degenerate and variant forms — the grammar scales down

- **1×2 matrix:** a single-position brand doing reach + distinctiveness. Still a matrix; still useful.
- **Stakeholder matrix:** territories × stakeholder-jobs for committee sales.
- **Phase matrix:** in strict campaign contexts, columns may be phases — acceptable only when the phase structure has an economic warrant, not as a calendar in disguise.
- **No matrix:** when territories or functions failed their invariant-level fit (one-film-a-year brand; crisis war-room), don't build one. A matrix with fake axes is worse than a list of decisions.

## Failure modes

- **The mural.** Built, admired, never consulted. Prevention: the proof hooks — if Module 9's reviews don't read the matrix, it is decoration.
- **Full-grid compulsion.** Filling every cell because empty cells look unfinished. Empty cells with reasons are the strategy.
- **Copied axes.** Rows or columns imported from a precedent case — the derivation test applies to axes above all.
- **Franchise sprawl.** Formats beyond capacity; the dead franchise signals decline more loudly than none.
- **Cell myopia.** Optimizing cells while the thesis rots — the matrix implements the strategy; it must never replace it.
